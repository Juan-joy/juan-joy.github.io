title: "Why I Think Every Engineer Should Work in Product"
date: 2025-08-15
description: "Reflections on switching between technical and commercial roles."
---

There's a particular kind of frustration that comes from building something technically excellent that nobody uses. I've felt it, and I think most engineers have too.

My time at Shopee as a product manager taught me something that no engineering course ever did: the gap between "this works" and "this matters" is enormous, and it's bridged by understanding people, not systems.

## The Translation Problem

Engineers are trained to optimise. We minimise pressure drops, maximise yields, reduce variance. But product work requires a different kind of optimisation - one where the objective function is messier, the constraints are human, and the data is never clean enough.

What I found was that my engineering training didn't hold me back in product. It gave me an edge. I could read a technical spec and understand the trade-offs. I could talk to developers without needing a translator. But I had to learn an entirely new set of skills around prioritisation, user research, and stakeholder management.

## The Case for Crossing Over

If you're an engineer considering a stint in product, business development, or any commercial role - do it. You won't lose your technical edge. You'll gain context that makes your technical decisions sharper.

The best engineers I've worked with understand *why* they're building something, not just *how*.
