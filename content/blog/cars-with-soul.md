There's a category of car that doesn't make sense on a spreadsheet. The Ferrari Testarossa is one. The original Honda NSX is another. They're not the fastest, not the most practical, not even the most beautiful by consensus. But they have something that most modern cars have engineered away: *character*.

## The Testarossa Problem

The Testarossa is absurd. It's too wide, too loud, and the side strakes look like someone took a cheese grater to a beautiful sculpture. But sit in one, fire it up, and you understand. The flat-12 behind your head isn't just an engine — it's an argument. An argument that excess, when committed to fully, becomes its own kind of elegance.

## The NSX Counter-Argument

The NSX is the opposite philosophy. Ayrton Senna helped develop it. It was engineered to be approachable — a supercar you could drive every day. Where the Testarossa overwhelms, the NSX communicates. Every input through the steering, every shift of the manual gearbox, tells you exactly what the car is doing and invites you to push a little further.

## Why This Matters

I think about this distinction a lot — not just about cars, but about how we build things. Do you want to overwhelm or communicate? Do you want spectacle or precision? The best answer, usually, is to pick one and commit fully. The worst thing you can be is forgettable.
